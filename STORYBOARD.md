# STORYBOARD

## Working title

**Matter to Count**

Subtitle: **A physical journey through arithmetic**

---

## 1. Purpose of the demo

This demo is not meant to be a generic math lesson or a dry software showcase.
It should feel like a short visual performance where arithmetic becomes physical, luminous, and spatial.

The viewer should come away with three impressions:

1. **Numbers have structure.**
2. **A base is a physical system of constraints and transformations.**
3. **Operations such as carry, borrow, shift, and accumulation can be seen, not just symbolized.**

The demo should work on two levels at the same time:

* immediate visual pleasure;
* delayed intellectual clarity.

---

## 2. Target duration

Recommended first cut: **2 minutes 30 seconds to 4 minutes**.

This is long enough to:

* establish the world;
* show several operations;
* create contrast between historical and digital visual languages;
* leave a strong final impression.

It is short enough to remain dense, memorable, and suitable for online sharing.

---

## 3. Narrative structure

The first version of the demo should follow a simple arc:

1. **Birth of number** — matter appears and organizes into columns.
2. **Addition** — accumulation and carry.
3. **Shift** — position becomes visible as value.
4. **Subtraction** — borrow as transformation.
5. **Multiplication** — repetition, offset, composition.
6. **Transition across eras and bases** — the same value persists through different worlds.
7. **Final synthesis** — arithmetic revealed as both ancient craft and modern machine logic.

---

## 4. Tone and visual rhythm

The tone should evolve across the demo.

### Opening

Slow, ceremonial, almost sacred.
The viewer must feel that something simple and fundamental is about to be revealed.

### Middle

More precise, kinetic, and satisfying.
Each operation should feel inevitable, mechanical, and elegant.

### Ending

Broader and more emotional.
The demo should suggest continuity between human calculation and digital computation.

The rhythm should alternate between:

* moments of stillness;
* local transformations;
* clean, decisive transitions.

Avoid constant motion.
The power of the demo comes from contrast.

---

## 5. Sequence-by-sequence storyboard

## Scene 0 — Black screen / first signal

**Duration:** 5–8 seconds

### Visual

A black screen.
A faint point of light appears in the center.
Then a second point.
Then a third.
They do not yet look like digits.
They feel like matter becoming countable.

Very faint dust or atmosphere may be visible.
No complex environment yet.

### Sound

A low, restrained hum.
Tiny discrete tones or impacts as each point appears.

### Purpose

Establish silence, gravity, and expectation.
This is not a menu. This is an emergence.

---

## Scene 1 — Columns emerge

**Duration:** 10–15 seconds

### Visual

The camera pulls back slowly.
The points align into vertical columns on a dark reflective plane.
Faint rails or grooves appear under them.
Column markers become visible: units, tens, hundreds.

At first, the markers may be abstract.
They do not need to be shown as explicit text immediately.

The space is still minimal, almost void-like.

### Motion

Objects settle into place with subtle precision.
The camera remains nearly frontal, with only a slight perspective.

### Sound

Light contact sounds.
A soft resonance as the rails lock into place.

### Purpose

Introduce the idea that number is positional.
The scene should say: these objects do not merely exist, they belong somewhere.

---

## Scene 2 — First addition without carry

**Duration:** 12–18 seconds

### Example

`12 + 15`

### Visual

Two rows of objects appear, one after the other.
The first row settles into the column structure.
The second row descends or slides into alignment.

Units merge with units.
Tens align with tens.
The result forms below or in a result line.

Everything resolves cleanly with no carry.

### Camera

Stable and readable.
A slight forward motion is acceptable as the result completes.

### Sound

Soft material clicks.
Pleasant but restrained.

### Purpose

Give the viewer a simple success case.
Show that the system is understandable before introducing transformation.

---

## Scene 3 — Addition with carry

**Duration:** 20–30 seconds

### Example

`17 + 8 = 25`

### Visual

The numbers appear in the same spatial grammar.
The unit column starts filling.
The last incoming object causes the column to overflow.

At that moment:

* the local result in the unit column resolves to the remainder;
* the excess value condenses into a luminous particle or beam;
* that carry rises, arcs, and lands in the next column.

Nearby objects briefly react to its passage.
A soft illumination spreads across the receiving column.
Then the final result stabilizes.

### Camera

Mostly strict.
A subtle tilt or close emphasis can happen only during the carry arc.
Then the camera returns to a clean readable framing.

### Sound

This is the first major sonic event.
Overflow should have a distinct sound:

* local compression;
* release;
* transfer;
* soft landing.

### Purpose

Reveal the core thesis of the project:
**carry is not a notation accident, it is a transfer of value.**

This is the first true “wow” moment.

---

## Scene 4 — Cascade carry

**Duration:** 15–20 seconds

### Example

`199 + 7 = 206`

### Visual

A denser addition.
The unit column saturates and emits a carry.
That carry triggers a second overflow.
Then possibly a third.

A chain reaction travels across columns.
The result only becomes stable at the very end.

### Motion

This should feel like controlled inevitability, not chaos.
Each carry is readable.
Timing matters more than spectacle.

### Purpose

Demonstrate that arithmetic has propagation.
A local event can reorganize a larger structure.

### Note

If the sequence feels too long, it can be shortened or merged with Scene 3 in a first cut.

---

## Scene 5 — Multiplication by the base / shift

**Duration:** 12–18 seconds

### Example

`27 × 10`
or, in binary,
`1011 × 2`

### Visual

The scene becomes more geometric.
All active objects slide by one column.
No explosion, no complex regrouping.
Just a pure displacement.

The beauty of the scene comes from how obvious the rule becomes.
The viewer should instantly feel:
**same symbols, new positions, new value.**

### Camera

Perfectly controlled.
This is a didactic scene.
Use the most legible angle in the whole demo.

### Sound

A smooth collective glide with slight impacts at the end.

### Purpose

This is one of the most important conceptual moments.
The demo explains positional notation without needing words.

---

## Scene 6 — Transition from decimal to binary world

**Duration:** 15–25 seconds

### Visual

The decimal environment begins to break apart.
The wood and stone structure fractures into planes, dust, or geometric shards.
The lighting cools.
A neon grid or metallic substrate emerges.

The number itself remains centered and preserved.
It does not disappear.
It is re-expressed.

Decimal beads or markers transform into metallic luminous binary units.
A familiar value survives an unfamiliar world.

### Sound

Warm acoustic tones dissolve into colder, more synthetic textures.

### Purpose

This scene is crucial philosophically.
It says:
**bases change, representation changes, matter changes — value remains.**

---

## Scene 7 — Binary addition

**Duration:** 15–25 seconds

### Example

`1011 + 0011`

### Visual

The binary world is cleaner, sharper, and more abstract.
Each active bit glows.
The addition proceeds with stricter visual logic.
Carries become pulses or narrow beams.

Because binary columns saturate quickly, carries appear often.
This creates a beautiful rhythm of local toggles and propagations.

### Motion

Minimal and crisp.
Less rolling, more switching and transfer.

### Sound

Sharper, more electronic.
Tiny pulses, toggles, and narrow-band transfers.

### Purpose

Show that the same arithmetic logic can become almost machine-like.
This scene should make developers smile.

---

## Scene 8 — Binary shift as pure geometry

**Duration:** 10–15 seconds

### Example

`01101 << 1`

### Visual

Bits slide left by one slot.
The operation is almost diagrammatic.
The result appears immediately cleaner and more architectural than decimal shift.

Optional: a faint grid trail shows the displacement path.

### Purpose

Create the strongest bridge between arithmetic and computation.
This scene should feel obvious, elegant, and modern.

---

## Scene 9 — Subtraction with borrow

**Duration:** 20–30 seconds

### Example

`52 - 7`

### Visual

The operation begins in a stable decimal environment, or in a hybrid environment if desired.
The unit column lacks enough matter.
A request propagates to the next column.
One higher-order unit descends.
Instead of merely moving, it transforms into a packet of lower-order units.

This is the key visual event:

* a ten becomes ten ones;
* a higher-order object decomposes into a lower-order swarm or aligned set.

Then the subtraction resolves.

### Camera

A slight close-up is justified when the borrow is transformed.
But readability must remain dominant.

### Sound

A deeper, more tactile sound than carry.
Borrow should feel like opening a sealed reserve.

### Purpose

Prove that subtraction is not “negative addition”.
It has its own visual logic: scarcity, request, decomposition, and settlement.

---

## Scene 10 — Multiplication as composition of rows

**Duration:** 25–40 seconds

### Example

A simple decimal multiplication such as:
`13 × 12`
or a binary multiplication if the first cut wants stronger visual economy.

### Visual

The multiplicand appears as a structured row.
The multiplier activates one row at a time.
Each partial product is formed.
Each row is shifted according to position.
Then the rows accumulate.

This should look like arithmetic becoming architecture.
Rows stack, align, offset, and finally collapse into a result.

### Motion

The pacing must remain readable.
Do not animate every micro-step if it hurts comprehension.
Prefer a few strong partial-product beats.

### Sound

Rhythmic and layered.
A good scene for more pronounced tempo.

### Purpose

Deliver the broadest and richest operation in the demo.
It should feel like the culmination of everything established earlier:

* position;
* accumulation;
* shift;
* carry;
* spatial composition.

---

## Scene 11 — Gallery of bases / one value, many worlds

**Duration:** 15–25 seconds

### Visual

A single value is shown through several worlds in rapid but graceful succession:

* base 60;
* base 10;
* base 16;
* base 8;
* base 2.

Each transition preserves the center while changing the surrounding material language.
The camera rotates slowly around the same mathematical object as its visible expression changes.

### Purpose

This is the conceptual synthesis scene.
It broadens the project from “cool arithmetic animation” to “visual philosophy of notation”.

---

## Scene 12 — Final tableau

**Duration:** 10–20 seconds

### Visual

The camera pulls back.
The different systems briefly coexist or leave traces of each other.
Carries, shifts, rows, and columns form a kind of frozen memory in space.

The final frame should be clean enough to work as a thumbnail or key visual.
The project title appears.

Possible final line on screen:

* **Matter to Count**
* **Arithmetic made visible**
* **From carry to computation**

### Sound

A final sustained chord or resonance.
Then silence.

### Purpose

Leave the viewer with the impression that arithmetic is older, stranger, and more beautiful than expected.

---

## 6. Optional narration or text overlays

The strongest first version may work **without spoken narration**.
Too much explanatory text would weaken the visual universality.

If text overlays are used, they should be rare and minimal.

Possible overlay lines:

* **Position gives value.**
* **Carry is transferred value.**
* **Borrow is transformed reserve.**
* **Shift is multiplication by the base.**
* **One value, many representations.**

These should appear only at major conceptual moments.

---

## 7. Editing principles

### Keep

* clean cuts;
* readable staging;
* emphasis on event timing;
* contrast between warm historical worlds and cold digital worlds.

### Avoid

* too many camera moves;
* excessive depth-of-field blur;
* flashy editing that fights the arithmetic;
* long sequences where the viewer no longer knows what is changing.

The edit should respect the internal dignity of the operation.

---

## 8. Production priorities for the first storyboarded prototype

If only a limited number of scenes can be produced at first, prioritize:

1. Scene 1 — Columns emerge
2. Scene 3 — Addition with carry
3. Scene 5 — Shift
4. Scene 6 — Decimal to binary transition
5. Scene 9 — Subtraction with borrow
6. Scene 10 — Multiplication

This reduced subset already expresses the project’s identity.

---

## 9. Core artistic rule

Every important event in the demo should satisfy at least one of these goals:

* clarify a mathematical idea;
* reveal a structural property of notation;
* produce a memorable physical transformation.

The best moments satisfy all three.

---

## 10. Final intent

The demo should feel like a meeting point between:

* ancient counting practices;
* algorithmic thinking;
* low-level graphics programming;
* and the pleasure of watching structure become visible.

It should not merely tell the viewer that arithmetic is elegant.
It should make that elegance impossible to miss.
